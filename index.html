About Us

Built by Someone Who Lived It

T.A.T.T.O.O. Foundation is a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt organization dedicated to combating labor trafficking within the trucking industry. We are survivor-led, trucker-focused, and unwilling to look away.

Who We Are

Truckers Against Trafficking Truckers & Owner Operators Foundation exists because no one else is addressing the labor trafficking happening to truckers. While the anti-trafficking movement focuses on training drivers to spot victims at truck stops, we recognize what others refuse to see: truckers themselves are being trafficked.

We are a labor advocacy organization building education, retraining programs, and exit infrastructure so that exploited drivers can reclaim their lives.

The Founder

Tanaha Koontz is a survivor of sex trafficking who entered the trucking industry seeking a fresh start. What she found instead were the same patterns she recognized from before: debt bondage, constant threats of homelessness for non-compliance, and coercion dressed up as employment.

She reached out to an existing anti-trafficking organization to raise these concerns. She was ignored. So she built T.A.T.T.O.O. Foundation to fill the gap that no one else would.

“I saw the poster that said ‘Do you want out of the life?’ And I did. Only I was no longer being trafficked for sex — I was now a labor trafficking victim performing as a trucker, and nobody could see what I was experiencing.” — Tanaha Koontz, Founder & Director of Operations

T.A.T.T.O.O. Foundation Is Not TAT

T.A.T.T.O.O. Foundation is not affiliated with Truckers Against Trafficking (TAT / tatnonprofit.org) in any way. TAT, originally founded as Chapter 61 Ministries, trains truckers to identify and report sex trafficking at truck stops. Their work focuses on truckers as observers of trafficking.

T.A.T.T.O.O. Foundation does something fundamentally different. We advocate for truckers who are trafficking victims — specifically labor trafficking victims — trapped within the industry itself through debt bondage, fraud, and coercion. We are not a ministry. We are not asking truckers to spot victims. We are saying the truckers are the victims, and the industry is the perpetrator.

The Problem

The Trucking Industry’s Hidden Trafficking Problem

Labor trafficking thrives in the trucking industry because the law allows it, the system ignores it, and the victims are invisible to the very movement that claims to fight trafficking.

Labor Trafficking in Trucking

The Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA) defines labor trafficking as the recruitment, harboring, or obtaining of a person for labor through force, fraud, or coercion (22 U.S.C. § 7102). In the trucking industry, these elements are structural:

Debt bondage through predatory training costs and lease-purchase schemes that leave drivers owing tens of thousands of dollars. Coercion through the constant threat of homelessness, loss of CDL, and career destruction for non-compliance. Fraud through systematically misrepresented pay, hidden deductions, and contracts designed to confuse.

These are not bad business practices. Under federal law, they constitute labor trafficking.

“The trucking industry doesn’t have a labor problem. It has a trafficking problem. The distinction matters because one has a legal remedy — if anyone would enforce it.”

The Legal Gap

The Motor Carrier Act (MCA) exemption to the Fair Labor Standards Act (29 U.S.C. § 213(b)(1)) strips truckers of basic labor protections that workers in virtually every other industry take for granted — including overtime pay.

The Department of Labor effectively will not assist truckers with labor violations because the MCA exemption creates a jurisdictional barrier. Drivers who have been defrauded, coerced, or trapped in debt bondage are told to hire their own attorneys — a financially impossible burden for people who are being exploited.

The system that is supposed to protect workers was designed with a carve-out that leaves truckers exposed.

The Invisible Victims

Survivors of sex trafficking who enter the trucking industry as a “fresh start” career often find themselves trapped in a new form of exploitation. They recognize the patterns — the debt, the threats, the isolation, the inability to leave — but this time, it’s called a job.

The anti-trafficking movement remains blind to this reality because it is fixated on sex trafficking. Existing organizations like TAT require mandatory training for CDL holders in states like Kansas — training that uses graphic video content depicting sex trafficking, retraumatizing survivors who are sitting in those orientations as new truckers.

No one asks the trucker in the orientation room whether they are a trafficking victim. The question is never directed inward. T.A.T.T.O.O. Foundation exists to change that.

What We Do

Programs

Knowledge is the first defense against exploitation. Exit strategy is the second. We are building both.

01

Education & Awareness

Survivor-informed training that equips truckers with the knowledge to recognize labor trafficking indicators in their own work arrangements — without retraumatizing anyone in the process.

  • Know-your-rights resources for CDL holders
  • Labor trafficking indicator identification
  • Legal rights under the TVPA and FLSA
  • Survivor-informed curriculum design
02

Retraining & Exit Strategy

The most powerful tool of coercion is the belief that there is no way out. We are building exit infrastructure so trapped truckers can leave the industry with marketable skills and a future.

  • Tech training and coding bootcamp partnerships
  • Career transition support and mentorship
  • Workforce development connections
  • Skills assessment and pathway planning
03

Housing & Stabilization

“Comply or be homeless” is the core coercion tool of labor trafficking in trucking. We are addressing it directly with housing support during career transition.

  • Housing support for exiting truckers
  • Stabilization resources during transition
  • Connection to existing assistance programs
  • Emergency support coordination
