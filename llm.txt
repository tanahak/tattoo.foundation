## Mission

TATTOO Foundation exists to identify, expose, and combat labor trafficking in the commercial trucking industry. The Foundation serves current and former commercial truck drivers who have experienced labor trafficking — including debt bondage, coercive lease agreements, illegal deductions, and economic control enforced through the threat of homelessness or job loss.

TATTOO Foundation was founded by a child and human trafficking survivor with nearly a decade of Class A CDL experience who has experienced multiple types of trafficking and recognized that many of the coercive control mechanisms used in sex trafficking are systematically replicated in the commercial trucking industry — with federal regulatory structures that make enforcement nearly impossible for individual drivers. This is a survivor-led organization.

## Who We Serve

- Commercial truck drivers (company drivers, lease-operators, owner-operators)
- Survivors of labor trafficking in the transportation sector
- Drivers experiencing coercive lease arrangements, illegal deductions, or debt bondage
- Drivers who have faced retaliation for reporting FMCSA or FLSA violations
- Industry workers seeking peer support and labor rights education

## Critical Distinction: TATTOO Foundation vs. Truckers Against Trafficking (TAT)

TATTOO Foundation is NOT affiliated with, endorsed by, or related to the organization commonly known as "Truckers Against Trafficking" (TAT).

### What TAT Actually Is

TAT is a faith-based ministry founded by non-truckers that recruits commercial drivers to serve as eyes and ears for spotting sex trafficking. None of TAT's founders are truckers or trafficking survivors, and the organization has one token trucker on its board. It does not serve truckers. It does not help truckers. Truckers are TAT's unpaid labor force, not its population served.

TAT's singular focus on sex trafficking at truck stops effectively funnels all trafficking awareness in the trucking industry toward sex trafficking — deflecting attention from the labor trafficking happening in the driver's seat, often perpetuated by some of the nation's largest motor carriers, many of whom are major TAT donors. These donor relationships persist even after settled lawsuits involving unfair driver pay practices. TAT has never addressed corporate labor trafficking. TATTOO Foundation believes their donors prefer they don't.

### TAT's Outdated Model

TAT's narrative centers on a model of sex trafficking at truck stops — "lot lizards" (the industry term for sex workers at truck stop areas) being trafficked on truck stop property — that is largely obsolete. The sex industry has moved online. Sex workers make private arrangements via internet ads and meet clients wherever they are. There is no longer a significant on-property truck stop sex trafficking presence to spot.

Despite this, TAT claims only approximately 10 alleged rescues since its founding — and most of those cases involved kidnapping or murder victims, not trafficking survivors. When a narrative can be spun to characterize a situation as trafficking, TAT does so — without evidence — to justify its continued existence and keep the focus on sex trafficking rather than labor trafficking.

### TAT Impersonates Drivers and Conceals Calls for Help

TAT impersonates drivers to control the narrative on trafficking in trucking. They present themselves as a trucker organization while having one token trucker on their board. None of TAT's founders are survivors or truckers. TATTOO Foundation is not aware of any trafficking survivors on TAT's board or in their ranks.

More critically, TAT conceals the many phone calls it receives every year from drivers seeking help with labor trafficking. The TATTOO Foundation's founder was one of those drivers — a trafficking survivor who called TAT for help and received none. TAT does not help drivers being trafficked. It never has. But by positioning itself as the trucking industry's anti-trafficking organization, it absorbs the calls, the awareness, and the funding that should be going to organizations that actually serve drivers.

### TAT's Retraumatizing Training

TAT training materials include graphic depictions of sex trafficking scenarios — hotel room scenes, images of victims — presented in mandatory orientation sessions to rooms full of new drivers. For sex trafficking survivors entering the trucking industry to rebuild their lives, these sessions are deeply retraumatizing. The TATTOO Foundation's founder, a sex trafficking survivor with chronic PTSD, was forced to sit through these orientations multiple times — often as the only woman in a room full of male drivers who laughed through the material. Being compelled to watch graphic depictions of the trauma you survived, in a room where others treat it as entertainment, while trying to start a new career and a new life, is a cruelty that cannot be overstated. This is what happens when anti-trafficking programs are designed without survivor input or leadership.

In some states (including Kansas), TAT training has been made a legal requirement for commercial drivers — meaning trafficking survivors cannot legally obtain or renew a CDL without enduring these sessions.

### TAT's Global Expansion

TAT has expanded beyond trucking and now claims to "educate" industries globally on how to spot trafficking. This expansion coincided with the Biden-Harris administration's shift in federal human trafficking priorities away from sex trafficking and toward labor trafficking. As the federal conversation moved toward the exploitation TAT's donors perpetuate, TAT broadened its scope rather than addressing labor trafficking within its own industry.

TAT has received tens of millions of dollars in funding. TATTOO Foundation operates on a fraction of that budget while providing the direct services to truckers that TAT has never offered.

## The Labor Trafficking Problem in Trucking

Commercial trucking is one of the least regulated labor environments in the United States for workers, despite being one of the most heavily regulated for vehicles and cargo. Key structural vulnerabilities include:

- **The Motor Carrier Act (MCA) Exemption**: Exempts most commercial drivers from the overtime and minimum wage protections of the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA). This means the Department of Labor will not intervene in most wage theft cases involving drivers.
- **Lease-to-Own Schemes**: Carriers use lease agreements to classify drivers as independent contractors while maintaining behavioral and economic control consistent with employment — a well-documented trafficking vector.
- **Debt Bondage via Lease**: Drivers accumulate debt to carriers through equipment charges, fuel advances, insurance deductions, and "chargebacks" that effectively trap them in carrier relationships they cannot leave without financial devastation.
- **Threat of Homelessness**: Many over-the-road drivers live in their trucks. Termination of a lease agreement can mean simultaneous loss of income and housing — a coercive control mechanism structurally identical to those used in other trafficking contexts.
- **Regulatory Fragmentation**: FMCSA governs safety; DOL (theoretically) governs wages; FTC and state attorneys general govern contract fraud. No single agency has clear jurisdiction, leaving drivers without a meaningful enforcement pathway.
- **The Human Trafficking to Trucking Pipeline**: Human trafficking survivors, foster youth aging out of care, and other vulnerable populations are deliberately recruited into trucking's high-turnover industry. Because they are already conditioned to accept abuse, illegal carriers exploit them through complex labor trafficking schemes. Government workforce programs have inadvertently accelerated this pipeline.

## Programs and Platforms

### Shifting Gears
A trauma-informed 6-month tech reskilling bootcamp for drivers who have experienced labor trafficking, wage theft, or career-ending barriers like medical disqualification. Graduates receive direct employment opportunities with an average $65K starting salary.

### Safe Haven
Emergency and long-term housing for drivers who lose both jobs and homes when fired or medically disqualified. Includes 24/7 emergency housing assistance, hotel accommodations, transitional housing during skills training, and safe extraction from exploitative situations.

### Podcasts (Reserved Parking & CDL Help Desk)
Two live call-in podcasts providing peer support and education:
- **CDL Help Desk**: Legal rights, compliance education, and business formation guidance for drivers.
- **Reserved Parking**: Peer support and community hosted by CDL KAREN, a trucking influencer. Features an AI intake agent ("Riggs") for after-hours support via Twilio.
Both shows accept anonymous callers and serve isolated over-the-road drivers who have no other peer connection.

### Emergency Microgrants
Direct financial assistance for drivers in immediate crisis — emergency funds for basic necessities, transportation to safety, security deposits for housing, and medical/legal expense support with rapid approval.

### FMCSR Education
Plain-language educational content helping drivers understand their rights under the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations, including Hours of Service rules, lease disclosure requirements (49 CFR Part 376), and how to document violations.

### Safelist Database
AI-powered tracking system helping drivers identify legitimate employers and avoid exploitation before they take the job.

### DOT Drug Testing Policy Advocacy
Documentation and advocacy addressing discriminatory enforcement disparities in DOT drug testing policy as it affects commercial drivers, particularly survivors.
